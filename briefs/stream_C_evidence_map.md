# Stream C — Mechanistic Plausibility: Structured Evidence Map

**GLP-1 receptor signalling in mesolimbic reward circuitry and neuropsychiatric outcomes.**
Purpose: plausibility only. This map does **not** produce an effect size and cannot
adjudicate the clinical safety question. It replaces the earlier directional
"reward-pathway dampening → therefore not harmful, therefore beneficial" argument with
a structured, bidirectional map that separates *target engagement* from *behavioural
consequence*, tags how far each finding sits from a real clinical endpoint, and makes the
**unstudied harm pathways explicit**.

## How to read this map

Each row is classified on the reviewer's axes:

- **evidence_level** — human / animal / cellular-molecular.
- **construct** — *target engagement* (is the GLP-1R expressed/occupied, and does its
  activation change mesolimbic signalling?) versus *behavioural/functional consequence*
  (reward, craving, impulsivity, anhedonia, mood, dopamine dynamics). Target engagement
  says the drug *can* act on the circuit; it says nothing about the *direction* of the
  behavioural outcome. Collapsing the two is the original overreach.
- **direction_supported** — supports-benefit / supports-harm / contradicts-benefit /
  contradicts-harm / neutral-or-context-dependent.
- **directness_to_clinical_outcome** — direct / indirect / remote, relative to
  suicidality or a real addiction endpoint. Almost all mechanistic evidence is *indirect*
  or *remote* to suicidality specifically.

## Contents of the map (28 rows: 25 verified primary/review citations + 3 explicit absence-of-evidence entries)

### Target engagement (n=7)
GLP-1R and its afferents are present in reward circuitry: preproglucagon/GLP-1 neurons in
the NTS project directly to VTA and NAc (PMID:22128031), receptor-expressing cells are
mapped across the brain (PMID:26500843), and central GLP-1R activation demonstrably
modulates cocaine-evoked phasic dopamine in NAc (PMID:28315693). In humans, GLP-1R
activation changes fMRI responses in appetite- and reward-related regions (PMID:25071023).
The canonical Gs/cAMP/PKA signalling pathway (PMID:29617641, 31767182) is direction-neutral.
**Crucially, target engagement is not a direction:** longer-term liraglutide *increased*
reward-related OFC activation at the weight-loss plateau (PMID:31282006), so the human
reward effect is time/context-dependent, not monotonic "dampening."

### Behavioural consequence — benefit side (n≈8, animal)
Regionally-specific rodent work links mesolimbic GLP-1R to reduced reward-seeking: VTA
GLP-1R signalling reduces alcohol self-administration (PMID:33043520); NAc/ventral
hippocampus/lateral septum reduce alcohol reinforcement (PMID:36480394); liraglutide
attenuates alcohol's reinforcing properties (PMID:26303264); VTA GLP-1R activation
attenuates cocaine seeking (PMID:29497166); exendin-4 reduces cocaine self-administration
(PMID:26072178), attenuates psychostimulant reward (PMID:23874851), and decreases
relapse-like drinking (PMID:28778739). This is the strongest, most internally consistent
part of the map — but it is animal, indirect to any human addiction endpoint, and about
*reward/reinforcement*, not suicidality.

### Context-dependence and contradicts-benefit (n=1 explicit + several neutral rows)
The benefit signal is **not universal**: GLP-1R agonism does **not** reduce abuse-related
effects of opioids (PMID:31058214). Opioid reward appears substantially GLP-1R-independent,
which by itself falsifies a simple "GLP-1R dampens all reward" story.

### Aversion / anhedonia-adjacent (n=3, neutral-or-context-dependent)
The same receptor drives an **aversive** axis. Part of GLP-1R-agonist food-intake
suppression is mediated by nausea/malaise/conditioned taste avoidance (PMID:22227019);
anorectic *and* aversive effects are mediated by brainstem CCK neurons (PMID:34844019);
and GIP co-agonism attenuates GLP-1R-agonist nausea/emesis (PMID:34380697), confirming a
dissociable aversive pathway. This matters because "reward dampening" and "aversive
internal state" are mechanistically adjacent — the circuitry that reduces craving can also
produce malaise, and whether that ever scales to clinically meaningful anhedonia/mood
worsening in vulnerable people is **not** answered here.

### Mood / emotionality — the pivotal bidirectional evidence (n=5)
This is where both directions coexist:
- **supports-harm signal (single, animal, acute):** GLP-1 is *both anxiogenic and
  antidepressant* — acute central GLP-1 raises anxiety-like behaviour while chronic GLP-1
  is antidepressant (PMID:26724568). A genuine, if narrow, mechanistic route to a
  short-term aversive/anxiety state.
- **contradicts-harm (animal):** liraglutide reduces depressive/anxiety-like behaviour in
  a corticosterone depression model (PMID:29705602).
- **contradicts-harm (human RCT, direct):** in MDD, semaglutide *improved* effort-based
  motivation (PMID:42054055) and did *not* worsen depressive severity and was reported safe
  (PMID:41218611).
- **neutral (human meta-analysis, direct):** across RCTs, GLP-1RAs modestly improved
  psychological well-being but had *no* effect on depressive symptoms, with any benefit
  judged likely **indirect** (metabolic/general health) rather than a direct mood mechanism
  (PMID:41914576).

### Explicit absence of evidence (n=3)
Three rows are deliberately included with citation = `UNSTUDIED`:
1. No mechanistic study tests whether GLP-1R activation directly drives **suicidality-relevant
   circuitry** (dorsal-raphe serotonergic, impulsivity/aggression). The proposed harm pathway
   is **unstudied, not refuted**.
2. Whether GLP-1R-mediated aversion/anhedonia signalling can, in vulnerable individuals,
   reach a clinically meaningful mood-worsening state is not directly tested — a plausible
   but uncharacterised harm bridge.
3. Rapid weight loss / caloric restriction as a mediator of mood change is not mechanistically
   dissected here.

## Why "no established mechanism for harm" is *not* evidence of safety

The literature offers a coherent mechanism for **reduced craving/reward-seeking** and, on
the mood side, evidence that leans against harm in already-depressed adults. It offers **no
established mechanism by which GLP-1R agonism would *increase* suicidality.** But the
correct reading of that gap is **absence of explanatory evidence**, not demonstrated
absence of effect:
- The relevant harm pathways (suicidality-specific circuitry; aversion→anhedonia escalation
  in vulnerable subgroups; weight-loss-mediated mood change) have **not been studied**, so
  there is nothing to have found.
- The one acute-anxiogenic finding (PMID:26724568) and the real aversive axis
  (PMID:34844019, 22227019) show the receptor is *capable* of aversive effects under some
  conditions — the direction is context-dependent, not settled.
- Mechanistic plausibility is not clinical evidence in either direction. Reward
  neurobiology can motivate hypotheses; it cannot substitute for Stream A designed-study
  effect estimates or Stream B pharmacovigilance.

## Calibrated plausibility verdict

The mechanistic evidence is **compatible with a reduction in reward-driven craving** —
GLP-1R is expressed in mesolimbic circuitry, its activation modulates NAc dopamine, and
region-specific agonism reduces alcohol and psychostimulant self-administration in rodents.
On the mood side, the limited human RCT and animal evidence leans **against** a
depressogenic/anhedonic effect, and there is **no established mechanistic pathway** from
GLP-1R agonism to suicidality. However, the same receptor drives a genuine **aversive**
axis, at least one study shows GLP-1 can be **acutely anxiogenic**, the benefit is
**not** uniform across substances (opioids excepted), and the specific harm pathways
relevant to suicidality are **unstudied rather than excluded**. Therefore: mechanism
offers a plausible basis for craving/reward reduction and **no established route to
suicidality**, but **mechanistic plausibility is not clinical evidence and cannot
adjudicate the clinical question in either direction.** The absence of a harm mechanism is
absence of explanatory evidence — it must not be read as evidence of safety.
